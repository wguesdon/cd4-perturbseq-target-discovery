**What do you want to build or investigate at this hackathon? Be specific about the problem, not just the topic area.**

Multi-omics profiling has become central to immuno-oncology. The bottleneck now lies in tertiary analysis — the layer where molecular signals are interpreted, integrated, and connected to biology. Much of the relevant knowledge sits in the published literature and in curated databases. Reconciling it with experimental results is slow, manual, and hard to scale.

The goal is to integrate prior biological knowledge and the published literature into biomarker discovery and patient stratification, anchored in checkpoint response (responder vs non-responder to anti-PD-1), likely in melanoma, as it maximizes retrievable literature and gives the cleanest label for a short build.

The work has three main objectives:

1) Knowledge integration. Using LLMs grounded on curated literature and databases through retrieval-augmented generation to contextualize multi-omics findings against existing knowledge, annotate candidate genes, proteins, and pathways, and surface relevant prior evidence while limiting unsupported claims.

2) Biomarker discovery. Applying LLM-assisted reasoning to prioritize and interpret candidate biomarkers across omics layers (genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics). Output will be traceable, literature-supported hypotheses for experimental follow-up.

3) Patient stratification. Using LLMs to interpret integrated molecular profiles and produce explainable characterizations of patient subgroups — linking molecular signatures to immune phenotypes or predicted response to immune checkpoint inhibitors.

The aim is not to replace established statistical and machine-learning pipelines, but to add a transparent, reproducible interpretation layer that connects their output to prior knowledge.
